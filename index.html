The Narrative

Documentary

This story takes place at 3,300m above sea level. Maras salt mine is an accumulation of more than 4,000 salt pools located high up in the vast Andes mountain range, 60 km from Cusco, Peru. Here, indigenous people have been collecting salt by hand for more than 600 years, before the Inca times. Today, the salt workers of Maras are owners of these “salt pools”, however, this wasn’t always like this.

7:00am at Marasal pools.

Historical reference

In the ’80s and 90’s the Peruvian government used to own Maras Salt Mines under a government company called EMSAL. During these years, the salt workers suffered unfair working conditions and wages for years and years. These decades were nothing but a struggle for the communities surrounding Maras Salt Mine, (Maras, Pichingoto and Moray) After weeks of protest demanding the government better working conditions, the salt production of Maras was handed to the municipality of Maras, in DEcember 1981. However, things didn’t change substantially.

Misty land, Marasal.

"Our documentary explores through Mrs. Laurita’s eyes"

After years of continued struggle, in 1997, the workers got the recognition of their ownership of the salt pools and today they proudly own and operate all of the salt production of Maras under a communal company founded by themselves called “Marasal”. There are more than 1,000 indigenous families in Maras that depend on salt production to make a living. The salt pools are inherited within the workers’ families, passing these land and knowledge generation after generation. It wasn’t until recent years after tourism boomed in the Sacred Valley of the Incas (Cusco to Machu Picchu) that the salt workers of Maras started to generate a living income for them and their families.

The salt Process

“This salt is like a vitamin, it has no disease,” Says Mrs. Laurita. Maras Salt comes from an underground creek from the mountain nearby. The salty water contained in these mountains runs and gets deposited in these “pools”, that have been built by the indigenous salt workers. After approximately 3 days and due to the heat from the sun, the water evaporates and leaves just salt, ready to be collected.

Cocina de Marasal, 2018.

Cocina de Marasal, 2018.

“This salt is like a vitamin, it has no disease”

The salt is later on collected by the salt workers and put into 50kg bags. In Maras Salt Mines, usually men carry these heavy loads on their shoulders, up and down the narrow paths between the salt pools, so they can put them into different storage units called “Eras”. Some workers start as early as 4 am, all the way until the sun goes down. The salt is later dried, packed, and transported to the local, national and in recent years, international markets. As Mrs. Laurita mentions in the film, this salt has no chemicals and it’s as pure as it gets. Similar to Himalayan rock salt, this salt has no “Mining” process and it remains as one of the only rock salt productions in the world to remain completely free of mining.

The Salt Workers

People start working at the salt mines from an early age. Mrs. Laurita has been working since she was 13 years old, and she has not stopped since. As mentioned above, during a normal workday, they collect the salt in 50kg bags, which are usually carried by the man, however, the collecting process is done by both men and women. Most of the salt workers come from the nearby communities of Pichingoto, Maras and Moray. Many of them walk the 4km mountain trek from Maras, however, some either take the transportation arranged by Marasal (Trucks) or some have their transportation as motorcycles.

Traditions

The Andean culture is very much present in the community of Maras. Often women will wear traditional clothing and hats while working and outside the workplace, however, men usually dress slightly more conservatively or what we could consider “Western”, unless for special occasions.

Cocina de Marasal, 2018.

Cocina de Marasal, 2018.

An interesting note is that different hats represent different communities in the region. For example, the length of Mrs. Laurita’s hat in the film is representative of the community of Maras. It is very normal during the day, that the workers will have a break and drink “Chicha” which is a traditional drink made out of potatoes. This keeps them energized and going through the hard and hot day of work.

Pachamama

The Andean vision of the earth is that Pachamama is our mother and we need to respect it. During our interviews with different salt workers, the conscious way of thinking is very much present in their everyday life and most of them respect mother nature in everything they do. During their daily life, but especially during August, they make offerings to Pachamama to bring better harvests, better weather and anything that mother nature can provide them with. Sometimes an offering to Pachamama can be as simple as pouring someone’s drink into the ground or bigger offerings such as burying pots with fully cooked food, next to coca leaves, wines and traditional drinks.

Tourism

In recent years, Maras Salt Mines has seen an increase in tourists per year. Since they opened for tourism a few years ago, they were able to collect a ticket for each tourist that comes. Today Maras Salt Mines receives more than 1.000 tourists a day. From our conversations with Leopoldo Zambrano, president of Marasal, he mentioned they only started to make a profit once tourism started in Maras Salt Pools. Before they would just break even every year.

With the help of tourism, Marasal has been able to improve the facilities in the salt pools, installing better bathrooms, pathways and kitchens for tourists and the salt workers. Also, they have permanently provided help to those in need in the surrounding communities, especially supporting young kids with education materials and others. The extraction of salt in Maras has become a tradition, a part of their culture, and today they are tourism-dependent to make a substantial profit.

Covid 19

With the current world situation of Covid-19, Maras Salt Mines remains closed for Tourism. A big percentage of the Maras population is over 60 years old so they are at big risk, financially and healthwise. The Future

“We would like to keep our cultures and traditions” Since the salt workers of Maras became owners of the salt pools, and years later managed to open for tourism, their quality of life has significantly improved. “Today we can send our kids to schools, to universities,” says Mrs. Laurita. With their income, the future generations don’t fully depend on the salt mines to make a living. The new generation of Maras can go to school and get educated. Many of them will go to bigger cities and some of them will stay. One thing is for sure, they will not be exploited the way their parents or grandparents were back in the days. Will this be one of the last generations working the salt mines? Things will keep changing and evolving at Maras Salt Pools, and some traditions might stay, some might get lost along the way.

Cocina de Marasal, 2018.

The people of Maras fought for many years for what they have, and this land belongs to them and their future generations. Our project and short film is a testament to who the Maras Salt Workers are today, where they come from and what is the future holding for them. It is our way of honouring their constant hard work for keeping their traditions alive, something that is harder and harder to find in the fast-changing world we live in. We are forever grateful for letting us tell their story and for touching our lives in such profound ways. Much love, Cristóbal, Tom & Toto.
